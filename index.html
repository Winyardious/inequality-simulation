Data sources & caveats (read me)

Household shares: long-run top-wealth-share series from Alvaredo, Atkinson & Morelli (2018), "Top wealth shares in the UK over more than a century"; recent levels cross-checked against the IFS Deaton Review and ONS Wealth & Assets Survey. Top 1% ≈ 70% (1914) → ≈ 16% (1980) → ≈ 20% (recent). The split of the top 1% into 0.1% / 0.9% is interpolated and the least certain part.

Why estimates vary: survey vs national-accounts aggregates move the top 1% by ~2 points (≈14.4–16.5% for 2016–18); the ONS survey puts the top 10% near 43%, capitalised series nearer 55–60%. Figures here are reasoned central values, not a single official number.

State waterline: ONS national balance sheet / IMF. UK public sector net worth is roughly −90 to −100% of GDP today (negative). The waterline along the bottom of the canvas is not a slice of the household pie — it is a different measure on its own scale, which is why it sinks below the baseline rather than shrinking a circle.

The 2026–2056 projection is an illustrative toy model, not a forecast. It assumes a tax only on hard-to-relocate bases so behavioural/capital-flight response is minimal. The state-rebuild uses a sourced revenue estimate (≈0.6% of GDP per 1pp of rate at low rates, with diminishing returns — calibrated from the Wealth Tax Commission and the Warwick/IFG ≈£24bn-from-2% figure, against ≈£2.6tn GDP). Its central assumption is that the revenue is banked into public net worth rather than spent on services — real governments mostly spend it, so this is a deliberate "what-if", not a prediction. The "≈1.7% holds the household line" figure is likewise a model assumption. All numbers live in the CONFIG block in the source.
